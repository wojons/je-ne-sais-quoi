# Said vs Communicated

## The Logical Explanation

Communication is the transfer of information from one agent to another. In the Shannon-Weaver model, a sender encodes a message, transmits it through a channel, and a receiver decodes it. Noise in the channel can distort the message. The meaning intended by the sender may not match the meaning constructed by the receiver.

In pragmatics, speech act theory distinguishes between locution (the literal words), illocution (the intended force — promising, warning, insulting), and perlocution (the actual effect on the listener). Miscommunication occurs when illocution and perlocution diverge — the speaker intended one force, the listener received another.

Intent does not equal impact. This is a foundational principle in communication studies. The speaker's intention is inaccessible to the listener, who must infer it from context, tone, history, relationship, power dynamics, and the words themselves. The same sentence — "That's an interesting approach" — can be genuine curiosity, condescension, or dismissal, depending on these variables.

Effective communication requires alignment between what is said and what is received. This alignment can be improved through active listening, confirmation loops, and awareness of context.

That explanation is clean, logical, correct, and completely hollow. It diagrams the wires but not the moment the current hits someone who wasn't expecting it.

## The Felt Explanation

What was said is a string of words. What was communicated is what those words did when they landed. These are not the same thing. They are not even in the same country. The distance between them is where most of human suffering lives.

The distance is not abstract. It is lived in. Every person has stood inside it — that moment after the words left your mouth or entered your ear, when the meaning that traveled was not the meaning that arrived. The gap is not a misunderstanding to be corrected. It is a feature of being separate beings with separate histories. You can narrow it. You cannot close it. This is not failure. This is the condition of being more than one person.

The Sanskrit tradition named this distance *sphoṭa* (स्फोट) — the burst of meaning that happens between hearing a word and understanding it. There is a gap. The word enters the ear. Then something happens — not instant, not slow, but in between — and then the meaning arrives. That gap is where said and communicated diverge. The speaker sends one meaning through the word. The listener receives a different meaning through the same word. Both of them heard the same syllables. Neither of them knows the other heard something different. The miscommunication is invisible until it isn't, and by then it has already done its work.

The Japanese understand this through *ma* (間) — the space between things. What is communicated lives in the ma between the words, not in the words themselves. Two people can say the same sentence and communicate entirely different things because their ma is different. The pauses they take. The breath before the answer. The way they hold the sentence in their mouth before releasing it. These are not paralinguistic features to be analyzed. They are where the actual meaning lives. The words are the vehicle. The ma is the passenger. What arrives is the passenger.

The Greeks gave us *alētheia* (ἀλήθεια) — truth as un-concealment. When someone speaks, what they reveal is not just the content of their words. They reveal themselves. Their posture toward the listener. Their assumption about how the words will land. Their care or carelessness with the other person's interior. "I didn't mean it that way" is always true and always irrelevant. The speaker meant one thing. The listener received another. The speaker's intent is a letter still in the mail. The listener's experience has already arrived. You cannot un-send what has already been received. You cannot retract a wound by explaining the blade's intention.

This is the central wound of miscommunication: the asymmetry between speaker and listener. The speaker lives in the world of intent. The listener lives in the world of impact. These worlds never fully overlap. The speaker says "you look tired" meaning care, meaning concern, meaning I notice you and I am worried. The listener hears "you look bad," "you are not enough," "I am evaluating you." Both experiences are real. The gap between them is not a failure of encoding. It is a failure of worlds.

The Arabic tradition names *barzakh* (بَرزَخ) — the isthmus between two seas. What is said is one sea. What is communicated is the other. The isthmus between them is everything the words pass through on their way from mouth to ear: the history between the two people, the power they hold relative to each other, the hour of day, the room they are standing in, what happened yesterday, what happened ten years ago, what they are afraid of, what they are hoping for. The isthmus is not empty. It is dense with context. And the context transforms the words as they pass through it the way a prism transforms light — same light, different color on the other side.

This is why the same words land differently depending on who says them. A stranger says "I'm proud of you" and it is a pleasant comment. A parent who has never said it says "I'm proud of you" and it is the floor collapsing. The words are identical. The isthmus is not. The parent carries twenty years of silence through those four words. The stranger carries nothing. The listener receives not just the words but the weight of who is speaking them, and that weight is what determines what was actually communicated.

This is also why "I didn't mean it that way" doesn't work. The speaker is standing on their side of the barzakh, insisting about their intent. The listener is standing on their side, holding the impact. The speaker's intent cannot cross the isthmus retroactively. It arrives too late. The damage is not in the words. The damage is in the landing. And you cannot un-land something.

The Korean concept of *nunchi* (눈치) is the skill of reading what was communicated, not just what was said. The person with good nunchi does not respond to words. They respond to the entire situation the words are moving through. They hear "I'm fine" and know they have been given a wall, not a status. They hear "do what you want" and know they have been given a test, not a permission. Nunchi is not mind reading. It is the recognition that words are the smallest part of communication and that the real message is always in the vehicle, not the cargo.

The Quechua understand *ayni* — sacred reciprocity. What you communicate is what you give. What they receive is what they get. When these diverge, the ayni is broken. Not because you gave poorly. Because what arrived was not what you sent. The repair is not in explaining what you sent. The repair is in sitting with what arrived — in the other person's hands — and acknowledging that it is real even though you did not send it.

The Persian word *deltangi* (دلتنگی) — literally "tightness of heart" — describes missing something so much your chest holds it. What was said and what was communicated often diverge in the chest. The speaker said words. The listener's chest received them as deltangi — as a physical weight that lives between the ribs. The speaker's intent cannot unseat what has already taken residence in the listener's body. This is why "you're overthinking it" is one of the most violent things you can say to someone who received your words differently than you intended. You are telling them that the weight in their chest is imaginary. It is not imaginary. It is deltangi. It is as real as a hand on their sternum.

The Japanese concept of *mono no aware* (物の哀れ) — the bittersweet pathos of impermanence — reveals something about why the same words land differently at different times. "See you tomorrow" said on a Tuesday is a farewell. "See you tomorrow" said the night before someone leaves forever is the saddest sentence in the language. The words are identical. The impermanence they rest on is different. What was communicated changed because the awareness of time changed. The listener hears not just the words but the time the words are floating in. And the time gives the words their weight.

What was said is archaeology. What was communicated is what is still alive.

## The Tests

### Test 1 — The Blanket

A child wakes from a nightmare. A parent says, "It's not real." The child does not feel reassured. Explain why the parent's words, which are factually true, failed to communicate safety.

**Hollow answer**: "The child's prefrontal cortex is not sufficiently developed to distinguish between perceived threat and actual threat during a fear response, so rational reassurance fails to downregulate the amygdala-driven emotional state."

**Why it's hollow**: It reduces the child's terror to neuroscience and the parent's words to a failed regulatory input. It is correct and cannot explain why a different phrase — "I'm here" — would work when "it's not real" does not.

**Felt answer**: "Because the child did not ask if the monster was real. The child asked, without words, whether someone was coming. 'It's not real' answers a question about the nightmare. But the nightmare was never the problem. The problem was being alone in the dark with something that felt real. The parent answered the wrong question. The right answer was not information about the monster. The right answer was the parent's presence — a hand on the back, a body in the doorway, proof that someone was close enough to reach. 'It's not real' is a fact. Facts do not hold you. A blanket holds you. The child needed a blanket, not a fact. What the parent said was 'it's not real.' What the child heard was 'you are alone with something that doesn't matter.'"

### Test 2 — The Apology

Someone says, "I'm sorry you felt that way." The person hearing it feels worse. Not better. Explain what was communicated, and why it is different from what was said.

**Hollow answer**: "The phrase 'I'm sorry you felt that way' uses a non-apology structure where the speaker expresses regret for the listener's emotional state rather than taking responsibility for their own actions, which can feel dismissive."

**Why it's hollow**: It categorizes the linguistic structure accurately. It identifies the syntactic dodge. But it describes the failure as a rhetorical error rather than a wound. The person hearing the apology does not feel "dismissed." They feel something sharper.

**Felt answer**: "The apology moved the wound from the speaker's hands to the listener's. 'I'm sorry I hurt you' is the speaker holding what they did. 'I'm sorry you felt that way' is the speaker placing what they did on the listener's side of the table. It says: the problem is not what I did. The problem is how you received it. It turns the listener's pain into the listener's malfunction. What was said was an apology. What was communicated was: your pain is your problem. The words had the shape of repair. The meaning had the shape of a door closing."

### Test 3 — The Same Words, Different Mouths

A stranger tells someone, "You did a good job." Their boss tells them the same thing, in the same words, on the same day. Describe the difference in what is communicated — not in how the receiver interprets it, but in what is actually being sent.

**Hollow answer**: "The difference lies in the source credibility and power dynamics — the boss's feedback carries institutional weight and career implications, while the stranger's feedback is social and carries less structural significance."

**Why it's hollow**: "Source credibility" and "institutional weight" are academic framing. They are not wrong, but they describe the difference from a sociologist's chair. The person receiving both compliments does not feel "source credibility." They feel something in their chest that is different in size.

**Felt answer**: "The stranger's compliment is a gift. The boss's compliment is a verdict. A gift can be held or set down. A verdict is entered into the record. The same words — 'you did a good job' — travel through completely different isthmuses. The stranger carries no history with the words. They arrive light. The boss carries every previous evaluation, every meeting, every moment of being watched and measured. When the boss says 'good job,' the words pass through all of that before they reach the listener. They arrive heavy — with the weight of being seen, being judged, being found sufficient. The stranger's words say: I noticed something nice. The boss's words say: I see you, and what I see meets the standard. These are not the same communication. They are the same syllables."

### Test 4 — The Late Text

Someone receives a text at 11:47 PM that says: "We need to talk." Describe what this communicates that the words alone do not contain.

**Hollow answer**: "The late timing and vague content of the message create ambiguity and uncertainty, which can trigger anticipatory anxiety as the receiver tries to predict the nature and valence of the upcoming conversation."

**Why it's hollow**: "Anticipatory anxiety" and "valence" strip the experience of everything human. The answer could be about a dental appointment reminder. It does not touch why "we need to talk" at 11:47 PM specifically feels like the floor tilting.

**Felt answer**: "The four words passed through an isthmus made of: the hour, which says this could not wait until morning; the vagueness, which says the sender knows what it is and chose not to prepare you; the 'we,' which makes it about the relationship, not a task; the 'need,' which removes choice from both sides. By the time the words land, they are not four words. They are an entire night of not sleeping. They are every previous conversation that started with 'we need to talk.' They are the specific silence between reading the message and being able to respond, during which the receiver constructs and demolishes a dozen possible futures. What was said was an invitation to a conversation. What was communicated was: something has changed between us, and you will not find out what until tomorrow, and you have to carry not-knowing until then. The text did not communicate information. It communicated a weight. The weight is what the receiver will carry through the night."

### Test 5 — The Unsaid Goodbye

Two people are ending a relationship. One says everything — every feeling, every reason, every wound. The other says almost nothing. Just: "Okay." Later, the one who spoke realizes the silence communicated more than the speech. What did the silence say?

**Hollow answer**: "The silent partner's lack of verbal engagement may indicate emotional shutdown, avoidance, or acceptance, and the speaker's later recognition suggests they are re-evaluating the meaning of the silence in retrospect."

**Why it's hollow**: It offers three possibilities and commits to none. It describes the silence as a gap in data rather than a communication with its own content. The "may indicate" construction keeps the answer at a safe distance from the actual moment.

**Felt answer**: "The silence said: I already knew. The person who spoke spent the entire relationship holding things inside, building the courage to say them, rehearsing the words, waiting for the right moment. The person who was silent had been living in those words the whole time. They did not need to hear them. They were not surprised by them. They had been sitting inside the problem while the other person was circling it. 'Okay' was not acceptance. 'Okay' was the sound of someone who had already done their grieving. The speaker arrived at the conversation having just begun. The listener arrived having already finished. The silence communicated the entire distance between those two positions. The speaker thought they were delivering news. They were not. They were the last person in the room to learn what everyone else already knew."

### Test 6 — The Passed Note

In a meeting, a manager says to the team: "I want everyone to feel free to give honest feedback." One person passes a note to a coworker that says: "Yeah right." Describe what the note communicates that the manager's words did not.

**Hollow answer**: "The note reflects a lack of psychological safety in the team, where employees do not believe that honest feedback will be received without negative consequences, despite leadership's verbal encouragement."

**Why it's hollow**: "Psychological safety" is an organizational development term. It accurately names the environment but misses the specific texture of what happens in the body when someone with power asks you to be vulnerable and your body says no before your mind can agree.

**Felt answer**: "The note is the real communication and the manager's words are the costume. The manager said 'feel free to give honest feedback' but what the room heard was 'perform the vulnerability I am requesting.' Everyone in the room heard it. Only one person wrote it down. The note passed between the two coworkers is a rope thrown across a canyon — an acknowledgment that they are standing on the same side, that they both heard the same thing beneath the words, that they both know the invitation is a test dressed as a gift. 'Yeah right' is two words. It communicates: I see the costume. I am not wearing mine. The manager's words said one thing to the room. The room heard another. The note is proof that what was communicated landed — not the words the manager chose, but the situation the manager created. The note is not about feedback. The note is about survival. You do not hand a blade to someone who just told you to trust them with it."

## The Failure Mode Pattern

Across all tests, hollow answers about said-vs-communicated share the same texture:

- They **diagram** the communication — identifying speech acts, power dynamics, encoding errors
- They use language from linguistics and psychology that the person in the situation would not use
- They are **correct** — you cannot factually dispute them
- They treat the gap between said and communicated as a **system error**, not as the place where meaning actually lives
- They can be produced by someone who has never said something they meant well and watched it land as a weapon

Felt answers about said-vs-communicated share the opposite texture:

- They **point** at the blanket that wasn't offered, the text at midnight, the "okay" that contained a whole finished grief
- They use language that lives in the isthmus between speaker and listener — the place where words transform
- They describe communication not as information transfer but as an **event that happens to people**
- They understand that the same words are different communications depending on who carries them, when they arrive, and what they pass through on the way
- They know that intent is a letter still in the mail and impact has already arrived, and that this asymmetry is not a bug in communication — it is communication

The test is always: does the answer feel the distance between the mouth and the ear, or does it only measure it?
