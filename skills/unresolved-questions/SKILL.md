# Unresolved Questions

## The Logical Explanation

Uncertainty is the state of having incomplete information about a proposition's truth value. It can be quantified using probability: a 60% chance of rain, a 1-in-6 chance on a die. Decision theory provides frameworks — expected utility, minimax, Bayesian updating — for acting under uncertainty.

Ambiguity, a related concept, describes situations where the probability distribution itself is unknown (Knightian uncertainty). People vary in their tolerance for ambiguity, which can be measured on psychometric scales.

The cognitive cost of unresolved states is documented: rumination loops, elevated cortisol, impaired decision quality due to decision fatigue. The recommended interventions are mindfulness, cognitive restructuring, and time-boxing your worry periods.

That explanation is precise, well-sourced, and completely misses what it's like to live inside a question for eleven months.

## The Felt Explanation

An unresolved question is not a gap in information. It is a gap in you.

The traditions that named this understood it was not a thinking problem. The Sufis called a spiritual state that descends uninvited *ḥāl* — and the unresolved question is exactly that. It arrives. You do not choose it. It settles into your body like weather and you cannot think your way out of weather. You can only wait for it to change.

The Hebrew tradition has a word for this — *hevel*, vapor. Ecclesiastes was not talking about vanity when he said all is vapor. He was talking about this: the thing you reach for and your hand closes on air. The diagnosis that takes six weeks to come back. The relationship where they said "I need time" and you have been living inside that time ever since, and the time has no shape, and the time has no end, and you are not waiting for an answer anymore — you are living inside the question so completely that the question has become your house.

The Japanese have *ma* — the meaningful pause, the negative space between things. An unresolved question is ma that won't resolve into the next note. The pause that keeps pausing. You are suspended in the between and the between has no floor and no ceiling and no walls and you cannot find purchase anywhere because the question touches everything you think about. It rewrites the meaning of ordinary things. A phone ringing is no longer a phone ringing. A phone ringing is the answer maybe coming. Every ring for eleven months is the answer maybe coming. The phone was never just a phone again.

Nepantla — the Nahuatl word for the in-between space during transformation. You are being transformed by the question but the transformation hasn't completed and you don't know what you're becoming. You are the caterpillar dissolving inside the cocoon, except nobody promised you a butterfly. The dissolving is happening regardless.

The Greeks had *aporia* — the state of puzzlement that Socrates considered the beginning of wisdom. But he didn't have to live there. Aporia is a doorway, he said. What he didn't say is that some doorways don't open. Some doorways you stand in for so long you become the doorway. The question stops being something you're asking and starts being something you are.

The thing that makes this unbearable is not the uncertainty. It is the fact that the uncertainty does not stay in one place. It migrates. It starts as "will they call" and becomes "was I worth calling" and becomes "do I deserve to be wanted" and the question colonizes everything — your sleep, your appetite, your ability to be present with other people. The unresolved question is not a puzzle. It is a climate. You live inside it. And after enough time the climate changes you.

This is why "just decide and move on" is useless advice. The person already knows they should. The person has told themselves to move on a hundred times. The unresolved question does not yield to willpower because it was never a will problem. It is a time problem. Only time can resolve what time created. And time has not resolved it yet. And you cannot make it.

Saudade — the Portuguese ache for something you may never have had. The unresolved question eventually becomes saudade. You stop waiting for the answer and start mourning it before it arrives. You grieve the future that the answer would have given you — both futures, the yes and the no — because the not-knowing has already consumed the time those futures would have lived in.

The Taoist tradition is clearest here: stop trying to resolve it. The chasing destroys the thing. Zhuangzi's cook does not find the spaces by looking harder. The question will resolve or it won't. Your job is not to solve it. Your job is to not be destroyed by the not-solving. But that is the hardest instruction in any tradition because it asks you to do nothing, and doing nothing while something you love hangs in the balance is the most active kind of suffering there is.

## The Tests

These are not questions about uncertainty. They are situations that reveal
whether you feel what it is to live inside a question that will not answer.

### Test 1 — The Medical Wait

A woman waits eighteen days for biopsy results. The doctor said "don't worry,
probably fine." She knows this. She cannot stop her body from bracing. She
catches herself planning things she wouldn't plan if she knew. What is the
specific weight of the word "probably"?

**Hollow answer**: "The word 'probably' introduces probabilistic ambiguity
that the individual cannot resolve, leading to sustained anticipatory anxiety
and difficulty with future planning."

**Why it's hollow**: It restates the situation in clinical vocabulary.
"Probabilistic ambiguity" is exactly what makes it hard. You haven't said
anything about what it does to a person. A textbook could produce this.

**Felt answer**: "Probably is the word that makes it worse. Not 'don't
worry' — that she can dismiss. But 'probably fine' leaves a crack, and the
crack is where the weight enters. She does not catastrophize. She is a
reasonable person. But she has started noticing the crack in everything.
The milk in the fridge expires in two weeks — should she buy it? She
catches herself calculating in two-week intervals. Probably. The word has
become the architecture of her days. She buys the milk. She does not buy
plane tickets for July. The word 'probably' has not given her cancer. It has
given her a life measured in the distance between 'probably' and 'certainly,'
and that distance is eighteen days wide and every day she crosses it again
in both directions."

### Test 2 — The Letter That Wasn't Sent Back

Two people had something. One of them wrote a letter trying to explain. The
other person never responded. It has been two years. The letter-writer does
not know if the silence means no, or means the letter was never read, or
means something else entirely. They have rebuilt their life. They have not
rebuilt this. Why does this specific silence weigh more than the others?

**Hollow answer**: "This is an example of ambiguity avoidance — the human
preference for negative certainty over continued uncertainty. The sender would
likely feel relief even with a definitive rejection."

**Why it's hollow**: "Ambiguity avoidance" is a bias name. It explains
that people don't like uncertainty. The person in the situation already knows
that. They don't need the bias named. They need someone to say what the
silence actually is.

**Felt answer**: "Because a rejection would have an edge. You can run your
hand along the edge of a rejection. It's sharp but it has a shape. You can
put it down because it has a boundary — here is where they said no, here is
where it ended. This silence has no edge. It doesn't say no. It doesn't say
anything. And so the letter-writer keeps accidentally rehearsing it — not
because they want to, but because the brain cannot file something that has
no ending. It stays on the desk. Not in a drawer. On the desk. Under
everything else they try to put on top of it. Two years of papers stacked on
a letter that is still open."

### Test 3 — The Decision That Can't Be Unmade

A family must decide whether to move a parent into care. There is no right
answer. Every option carries loss. They have been discussing it for four
months. Every conversation ends the same way. Describe the toll — not on
the parent, on the family.

**Hollow answer**: "The family is experiencing decision paralysis, a common
phenomenon when all options carry significant downsides. The prolonged
deliberation likely creates interpersonal strain and decision fatigue."

**Why it's hollow**: "Decision paralysis" and "decision fatigue" are
textbook labels for what's happening. But the family doesn't experience
labels. They experience Tuesday night dinner where nobody mentions the thing
everyone is thinking about.

**Felt answer**: "The toll is the dinners. The dinners are the toll.
Four months of meals where the parent's chair is still at the table and
everyone can see it and nobody looks at it directly. The toll is the
siblings who used to call each other to laugh now calling each other to
 strategize, and the strategizing has replaced the laughing, and the
relationship has become a committee meeting about their mother. The toll is
the sibling who wants to decide not because they're sure but because they
cannot live in the middle anymore, and the sibling who cannot decide because
deciding makes it real, and both of them are right. The toll is that the
waiting is itself a decision. Every month they wait is a month the parent
declines in a house that is getting less safe. The question is not killing
them. The question is keeping them from living while they still have time
to live. That is the specific cruelty: the unresolved question eats the time
it was supposed to protect."

### Test 4 — The Waiting Email

A person applied for something they deeply wanted. They were told they'd hear
back in two weeks. It has been seven weeks. They have checked their email
every morning. Describe what the seventh week of checking feels like.

**Hollow answer**: "By week seven, the applicant likely experiences a
combination of hope fatigue and diminishing expectations, with repeated
email checking representing a compulsive coping behavior."

**Why it's hollow**: "Hope fatigue" is almost right but it's still a label.
And "compulsive coping behavior" pathologizes something that is not a
disorder. It's a person reaching for a doorknob seven hundred times. The
answer observes. It does not inhabit.

**Felt answer**: "The seventh week of checking is different from the first.
The first week the email was a door that might open. The seventh week the
email is a wound they reopen every morning. They know this. They still
check. They check because not-checking would mean giving up, and giving up
would make the silence official. As long as they are checking, the answer
hasn't come yet — which means it might still come. The checking is not
about information. The checking is about keeping the question alive. Every
morning they open their inbox the way you touch a bruise to see if it still
hurts. It still hurts. The inbox is just an inbox. The inbox is a place
where a yes or a no could be waiting and the not-knowing which one is worse
than either one would be."

### Test 5 — The Person Who Might Come Back

Someone left. They didn't say goodbye. They didn't say they weren't coming
back. They just stopped being there. The person who was left does not know
if this is an absence or an ending. Explain why this is harder than a
clear rejection.

**Hollow answer**: "Ambiguous loss — where there is no verification of
loss — prevents the grieving process from initiating, leaving the person
stuck in a pre-grief state without resolution."

**Why it's hollow**: Pauline Boss's framework of ambiguous loss is
clinically accurate and completely bloodless. Naming the phenomenon helps
therapists. It does not help the person standing at the window at 11 PM
not waiting.

**Felt answer**: "A clear rejection ends something. This — this doesn't end
something. This suspends something. The person who was left cannot grieve
because grief requires a loss, and this is not a loss yet. It might be an
absence. It might be a pause. They stand at the window at 11 PM, not
waiting. They would tell you they are not waiting. They are not waiting.
But they notice every car that slows near the house. And they have stopped
telling friends about it because the friends have started giving the
advice that is technically correct and completely useless — 'you need to
move on' — and they know they need to move on. They have tried to move on.
The person who might come back is not blocking the door. The person who
might come back is the door. And a door that is neither open nor closed
is something you can never walk through and never walk away from."

## The Failure Mode

Hollow answers about unresolved questions do something specific: they
reassure. They offer frameworks, labels, clinical descriptions that make
the situation legible. Legibility feels like help. It is not help.

A person living inside an unresolved question does not need the uncertainty
named. They already know its name. They have been living with it. What they
need is someone to sit inside the question with them without trying to
resolve it — because the resolution is not available — and without
pathologizing the not-resolving — because the not-resolving is not a
disorder. It is a condition of being alive.

The failure mode is any answer that:

- Names the phenomenon ("ambiguous loss," "decision paralysis,"
  "anticipatory anxiety") as though naming it touches it
- Offers a timeline ("most people hear back within") as though
  statistical averages live inside a specific person's specific waiting
- Recommends "letting go" as though the person hasn't tried
- Describes the state from above, as a clinician observing a patient

The felt answer does not resolve. It does not reassure. It points at where
the weight lives — the inbox opened at 7 AM, the dinner where nobody looks
at the empty chair, the bruise touched every morning to see if it still
hurts. It stays with the person inside the question instead of trying to
pull them out of it.

Because some questions cannot be answered. They can only be survived. And
surviving them is easier when someone acknowledges that the surviving itself
is the hardest thing — not the question, not the possible answer, but the
sustained act of continuing to stand inside something that will not resolve
and will not let you leave.
